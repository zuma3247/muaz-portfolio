Personal Leadership Project
Part 1 – Insights from Class Activities
Throughout the semester we dived into a variety of leadership exercises. Every activity felt interesting and genuinely thought provoking; it was fascinating to discover new facets of myself and to learn how my classmates see the world. Some assessment results were spot on, while others had me asking, “huhhhhh?”, which was a healthy reminder that leadership growth comes with both clarity and surprise.
Spot on moments. On the SLPI 360 my observers rated me a perfect 5.0 on “Fosters cooperative relationships” and “Treats people with respect,” confirming my natural people oriented tendency to create safe, collaborative spaces.
Head tilt moments. The same inventory showed me lagging in “Inspire a Shared Vision,” yet my peers scored me almost ten points higher than I scored myself. Realising that I already project conviction more strongly than I notice revealed an emerging task orientation I’ve been underestimating.
Meanwhile, our readings and class discussions on the spectrum of people oriented versus task oriented leadership deepened my understanding of effectiveness. I now see great leadership as walking with the team (people) while clearing the trail ahead (tasks). 
Key Take Aways
1.	Humility plus evidence. I tend to underrate myself, so I’ll seek feedback before judging my impact.
2.	Balanced style. Effective leadership for me means walking with people while clearing the trail.
3.	Voice the vision sooner. Since others already hear conviction, I should trust that voice and articulate purpose earlier.






Part 2 – Personal Leadership Philosophy
“No one should follow me. I’m human. I’m prone to error and forgetfulness. But I invite everyone to walk with me so we can progress together.”
Leadership is a shared journey that blends relationships and results. Five actionable core values drawn from my Values Statement anchor how I lead:
•	Practice Empathy – understand before guiding.
•	Cultivate Accountability – own promises and create a culture where others can, too.
•	Pursue Excellence – chase improvement, not perfection, in every deliverable.
•	Honor Loyalty – protect trust and stand by teammates, especially under pressure.
•	Drive Innovation – shatter expectations by experimenting boldly and learning fast.
Key Behaviours
1.	Listen, then frame the “why.”
2.	Co create clear, doable steps (task orientation).
3.	Hand over the mic—share autonomy and recognition (people orientation).
4.	Celebrate wins and failures as learning fuel.
Why walk with me? I take calculated risks, share credit, and refuse to pretend that I’m perfect. Together, we move farther than any one of us could alone.








Part 3 – Why This Statement Matters & How It Will Guide Me
Importance of Each Core Value
•	Empathy roots my faith and community roles; leadership without it feels hollow.
•	Accountability ensures promises turn into outcomes.
•	Excellence breeds success.
•	Loyalty reflects my commitment to long term relationships, from club members to peers.
•	Innovation fuels curiosity and positions me to tackle emerging challenges in design and technology.
Guiding Compass for What’s Ahead
My five core values act less like a rulebook and more like a compass:
•	Empathy – start by grasping how decisions affect real people.
•	Accountability – convert intentions into owned commitments.
•	Excellence – nudge every idea one step better than yesterday.
•	Loyalty – guard relationships so trust holds under pressure.
•	Innovation – stay curious enough to redraw the map when needed.
When I face an unfamiliar challenge, I’ll pause and ask: Does this option honor empathy, accountability, excellence, loyalty, and innovation? If the answer is "yes," I know I’m heading in the right direction.
